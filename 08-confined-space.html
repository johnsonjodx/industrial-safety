üìå Introduction to Confined Space Entry

Confined spaces are among the most dangerous work environments. Each year, workers die in confined spaces from oxygen deficiency, toxic atmospheres, engulfment, and other hazards. Often, would-be rescuers become victims themselves, making confined space incidents particularly deadly.

Stark Reality: According to OSHA, approximately 60% of confined space fatalities are would-be rescuers. Never attempt rescue without proper training and equipment.

A confined space is any space that is large enough for a worker to enter, has limited or restricted means of entry and exit, and is not designed for continuous occupancy. Examples include tanks, silos, storage bins, hoppers, vaults, pits, manholes, tunnels, equipment housings, and pipelines.

This module covers the critical aspects of confined space safety, including identification, hazard assessment, atmospheric testing, permit systems, entry procedures, and emergency response.

1. Confined Space Classification

Not all confined spaces are the same. OSHA classifies confined spaces into two categories based on the presence of hazards.

A. Non-Permit Required Confined Space

‚úì NON-PERMIT CONFINED SPACE

A confined space that does not contain or have the potential to contain any hazards capable of causing death or serious physical harm.

Characteristics:

  • No actual or potential atmospheric hazards
  • No engulfment hazards
  • No internal configuration hazards (like sloping walls that could trap)
  • No other serious safety hazards

While these spaces don't require a permit, they still require hazard assessment and safe work practices.

B. Permit-Required Confined Space (Permit Space)

‚ö†Ô∏è PERMIT-REQUIRED CONFINED SPACE ‚ö†Ô∏è

Contains or has potential to contain serious hazards. Requires written permit, trained entrants/attendants, and specific procedures.

A permit-required confined space has one or more of these characteristics:

Hazard Type Description Examples Consequences
Atmospheric Hazards Hazardous air contaminants, oxygen deficiency/enrichment, flammable atmosphere H2S, CO, solvent vapors, methane, low O2 Asphyxiation, poisoning, explosion
Engulfment Hazards Material that can surround and trap a worker Grain, sand, liquids, sewage, powders Suffocation, crushing
Configuration Hazards Internal shape that could trap or asphyxiate Converging walls, sloping floors, hoppers Entrapment, inability to escape
Other Hazards Any other serious safety hazard Mechanical equipment, electrical, heat, noise, falling objects Crushing, electrocution, burns, impact

C. Reclassification

A permit space can be reclassified as a non-permit space if:

  • All hazards have been eliminated (not just controlled)
  • Documentation shows how hazards were eliminated
  • No entry is made for any other purpose

If hazards are only controlled (e.g., ventilation), the space remains a permit space.

2. Atmospheric Hazards and Testing

Atmospheric hazards are the most dangerous and least visible confined space threats. Proper testing is essential.

A. Oxygen Levels

Oxygen Level Classification Hazards Action
< 19.5% Oxygen Deficient Impaired judgment, unconsciousness, death DO NOT ENTER - ventilate, find source
19.5% - 23.5% Safe Range Normal breathing, safe for entry Acceptable for entry
> 23.5% Oxygen Enriched Increased fire risk, materials ignite easily DO NOT ENTER - find source

B. Flammable Atmospheres

LEL Percentage Classification Risk Action
0% No flammable gas Safe for entry Continue monitoring
1-9% Trace amounts Potential hazard if source continues Investigate source, ventilate
10%+ Flammable Explosion risk DO NOT ENTER - ventilate, find source

C. Toxic Gases

Common toxic gases in confined spaces:

Gas Source PEL (OSHA) IDLH Effects
Hydrogen Sulfide (H2S) Sewage, petroleum, decomposing matter 10 ppm 100 ppm Rotten egg smell (then odorless), paralysis of smell, death
Carbon Monoxide (CO) Combustion engines, heaters, welding 50 ppm 1,200 ppm Odorless, colorless, binds to blood, causes headache, death
Chlorine Water treatment, chemical processes 0.5 ppm 10 ppm Respiratory irritation, pulmonary edema
Ammonia Refrigeration, fertilizers, cleaning 50 ppm 300 ppm Respiratory irritation, burns
Methane Decomposition, natural gas N/A (asphyxiant) N/A Displaces oxygen, explosive

D. Atmospheric Testing Protocol

Testing must be done in this order:

  1. Oxygen: Test first because it affects other readings
  2. Flammability: Test second because oxygen affects readings
  3. Toxic gases: Test third, specific to expected contaminants

Testing Requirements:

  • Test at all levels (top, middle, bottom) because gases stratify
  • Continuous monitoring while occupied
  • Calibrate monitors before each use
  • Document all test results on permit
  • Retest if conditions change or after breaks
Critical: Hydrogen sulfide is heavier than air and accumulates at the bottom. Methane is lighter than air and accumulates at the top. Test ALL levels.

A. Entrant Responsibilities

  • Know the hazards that may be faced during entry
  • Properly use equipment (PPE, monitoring, communications)
  • Maintain continuous communication with attendant
  • Alert attendant of warning signs or hazardous conditions
  • Exit as quickly as possible when ordered, when alarm sounds, or when aware of danger

B. Attendant Responsibilities

  • Remain outside the space at all times during entry
  • Know the hazards and recognize signs of danger
  • Maintain accurate count of entrants
  • Monitor activities inside and outside the space
  • Order evacuation when necessary
  • Summon rescue services when needed
  • Perform non-entry rescues if trained and equipped
  • No other duties that could distract from monitoring

C. Entry Supervisor Responsibilities

  • Verify entry permit is complete and accurate
  • Confirm all tests have been conducted and are acceptable
  • Verify all procedures and equipment are in place
  • Terminate entry and cancel permit when work is complete
  • Remove unauthorized individuals
  • Ensure rescue services are available
NEVER: The attendant may never enter the space to attempt rescue. This is the leading cause of multiple fatalities. Wait for trained rescuers.

4. Permit System and Entry Procedures

The entry permit is a written document that authorizes entry and ensures all precautions have been taken.

A. Permit Requirements

OSHA requires permits to include:

  • Space identification
  • Purpose of entry
  • Date and duration of permit
  • Authorized entrants (names)
  • Attendant(s) and supervisor
  • Hazards of the space
  • Isolation methods used
  • Atmospheric test results
  • Rescue services and methods
  • Communication procedures
  • Equipment required (PPE, monitoring, lighting)
  • Signature of entry supervisor
üìã SAMPLE PERMIT ENTRIES:
Space: Reactor Vessel R-101
Date: 02/17/2025 | Time: 08:00 - 16:00
Entrants: J. Smith, M. Jones
Attendant: R. Brown
Supervisor: T. Wilson
O2: 20.9% | LEL: 0% | H2S: 0 ppm | CO: 0 ppm
Isolation: Blanked, Locked out
PPE: Full face respirator, coveralls, gloves
Communication: Radio channel 3

B. Entry Procedures

Before Entry:

  1. Identify and evaluate hazards
  2. Isolate the space (lockout/tagout, blanking, disconnects)
  3. Purge, inert, flush, or ventilate as needed
  4. Conduct atmospheric testing (top to bottom)
  5. Complete entry permit
  6. Review permit with all team members
  7. Stage rescue equipment
  8. Verify communication systems

During Entry:

  1. Continuous atmospheric monitoring
  2. Continuous communication between entrant and attendant
  3. Attendant maintains visual contact when possible
  4. Monitor for changing conditions
  5. Rotate entrants as needed (fatigue)

Terminating Entry:

  1. Remove all entrants and equipment
  2. Close and secure the space
  3. Cancel permit
  4. Review entry for lessons learned

C. Isolation Methods

Method Description Application
Blank/Blind Solid plate inserted between flanges Pipes, ducts, lines entering space
Double Block and Bleed Two valves closed, vent open between them Piping systems, ensures no flow
Lockout/Tagout Lock energy-isolating devices Electrical, mechanical, hydraulic energy
Disconnect Physically separate piping or wiring Positive isolation, highest reliability
Chocking/Blocking Prevent movement of parts Elevated parts, gravity hazards

5. Ventilation and Personal Protective Equipment

Proper ventilation and PPE are critical for safe confined space entry.

A. Ventilation Requirements

  • Mechanical ventilation: Required for all permit spaces unless atmosphere is safe without it
  • Air movement: Must provide at least 20 air changes per hour
  • Duct placement: Supply air should be placed to sweep all areas, exhaust near contaminant source
  • Monitoring: Continuously monitor to ensure ventilation is effective
  • Backup: Have backup ventilation in case of failure

B. PPE for Confined Spaces

PPE Type When Required Considerations
Respiratory Protection Any atmospheric hazard, or if ventilation inadequate SCBA or airline respirator with escape bottle for IDLH
Head Protection Always in industrial confined spaces Hard hat with chin strap (looking up/down)
Body Protection Chemical hazards, abrasion, heat/cold Coveralls, chemical suits, flash suits
Hand Protection Chemical, cut, abrasion hazards Chemical gloves, cut-resistant liners
Foot Protection Always Steel-toe, chemical-resistant if needed
Fall Protection Vertical entry, elevated work Harness, lanyard, retractable, tripod/winch
Retrieval Equipment All permit spaces Harness, lifeline, mechanical retrieval device

C. Retrieval Systems

OSHA requires retrieval systems for all permit space entries:

  • Full-body harness: Worn by entrant with D-ring centered on back
  • Lifeline: Attached to harness, extends to outside
  • Mechanical retrieval device: Tripod/winch or davit arm for vertical entry
  • Attendant: Must be able to operate retrieval system
Exception: If retrieval equipment would create a greater hazard or not assist rescue, alternative methods must be documented on permit.

6. Rescue and Emergency Response

Rescue planning is not optional‚Äîit's required before any entry.

A. Rescue Requirements

  • Employer must ensure rescue services are available and capable
  • Rescuers must be trained in confined space rescue
  • Rescuers must practice annually
  • Rescue equipment must be available and maintained
  • Rescue must be initiated immediately if needed

B. Types of Rescue

Rescue Type Description Requirements When Used
Non-Entry Rescue Entrant removed from outside using retrieval line Harness, lifeline, mechanical retrieval device First choice, fastest, safest
Entry Rescue Rescuers enter to retrieve victim Trained rescuers, backup team, all PPE When non-entry fails or impossible
Self-Rescue Entrant exits on own Awareness, ability, clear path When entrant detects problem early

C. Emergency Procedures

  1. Attendant recognizes emergency (alarm, loss of communication, visible distress)
  2. Attendant orders evacuation and attempts non-entry rescue
  3. Attendant summons rescue services immediately
  4. No entry by untrained personnel - wait for trained rescuers
  5. Provide information to rescuers (hazards, number of victims, last known location)
NEVER FORGET: 60% of confined space fatalities are would-be rescuers. Do NOT enter to attempt rescue unless you are trained, equipped, and part of the official rescue team.

Key Takeaways:

  • Confined spaces have limited entry/exit and are not for continuous occupancy
  • Permit spaces have actual or potential hazards
  • Test atmosphere in order: Oxygen, Flammability, Toxics
  • Oxygen must be 19.5-23.5% for safe entry
  • Entry team: Entrant, Attendant, Entry Supervisor
  • Attendant NEVER enters the space
  • Continuous monitoring and communication required
  • Rescue must be planned before entry
  • Non-entry rescue is the first choice

üéØ Conclusion

Confined spaces are unforgiving environments where mistakes can be fatal. Unlike many workplace hazards that give warnings, atmospheric hazards are often invisible and can overcome workers in seconds. The complexity of confined space safety‚Äîfrom proper classification to atmospheric testing to rescue planning‚Äîrequires rigorous attention to detail and unwavering commitment to procedures.

The key principles of confined space safety are:

  • Never assume - Test and verify every time
  • Follow the permit - It exists to keep you alive
  • Stay in communication - Attendant is your lifeline
  • Don't become a victim - Never attempt untrained rescue
  • Continuous monitoring - Conditions can change instantly

Remember: The confined space doesn't care if you've entered safely a hundred times. It only takes one mistake, one unexpected condition, one moment of complacency to turn fatal.

Key Takeaways from This Module:

  • Permit spaces have atmospheric, engulfment, configuration, or other serious hazards
  • Test oxygen first, then flammability, then toxics
  • Safe oxygen range: 19.5% - 23.5%
  • Flammable gas must be <10% LEL for entry
  • Entry team: Entrant (inside), Attendant (outside), Supervisor (authorizes)
  • Attendant never enters the space - even in emergency
  • Continuous monitoring and communication required
  • Non-entry rescue is always the first choice
  • 60% of fatalities are would-be rescuers
